§ 01 — What We Believe
India doesn't imitate anything.
It leads something.

Not Indian fashion. Contemporary Indian design. The distinction is intent.

For decades, Indian designers operated in one of two modes: heritage — celebrating the artisan, looking backward — or aspiration, looking toward Paris for what "modern" was supposed to mean. Both were deferential. Both assumed the reference point was elsewhere.

That moment is over. Kartik Research on Orchard Street. Péro in 350 stores across 35 countries. Dhruv Kapoor at 40+ global stockists. Suket Dhir, International Woolmark Prize winner. The evidence is overwhelming.

And-a-Man is the platform that was always missing — the one that treats Indian design with the same editorial seriousness that Colette gave French design, that Dover Street Market gave conceptual fashion.

"And-a-Man is where Indian design stops explaining itself and starts setting the terms."
The new Indian expression · For those who recognise it
§ 02 — The Three Lenses
Every brand we carry
must pass three tests.
01
Made with Intelligence
The Quality Floor
The product reflects real material and process knowledge. Handloom. Natural dyes. Textile innovation. Precise construction.
Never generic fabric with no thinking behind it.

This sets the quality floor — not the identity. We are not saying every brand must be craft-based. We are saying every brand must know what it's made of — and why.

A designer using technical fabric with intent passes. Using polyester because it's cheap does not.
The test Ask: why this fabric, why this process? If the answer is specific and reveals material thinking — they're in. If it's generic — they're not ready.
02
Wears like the World
The Silhouette Standard
Every piece must feel current. Wearable in a wardrobe that includes Lemaire, Auralee, Loewe.
Not occasion-wear. Not costume. Clothes for real life — Mumbai on a Tuesday, London at a gallery, New York at dinner.

This is not about looking Western. It's about looking now.

The best Indian designers aren't imitating. They're building silhouettes that belong to the present — using materials and knowledge that are uniquely theirs. That combination is still rare. That's the opportunity.
The test Does this piece sit naturally with the best global labels — not as "ethnic," but as an equal? If yes — we're interested. If it only works at a wedding — it's not for us.
03
Sounds like India
The Voice Test
The designer's point of view is irreducibly Indian. Not through motifs or materials — but through how they see proportion, colour, and the body.

No apology. No nostalgia. No imitation. Just a clear signal: this came from here — and here matters.

This is the hardest lens to define, and the easiest to feel.

Remove the label. Is India still in the room?
Not as decoration — as a way of thinking.
The test Remove the label. Remove the story. Does the garment itself carry an Indian design intelligence? If yes — it belongs. If it needs explanation — it doesn't.

§ 04 — The Practical Filter
Every brand must pass
six filters.
01
Material intelligence
Fabric is the thinking. Why this material, this process? The answer must be specific. Vague answers fail immediately.
02
Wearability
Can it live beyond the occasion? A Lemaire customer on a Mumbai Tuesday is the test. If it only works at a wedding, it isn't for us.
03
Indian POV
Remove the label. Is India still in the room? If yes — it belongs. If not — it doesn't.
04
Design clarity
A genuine point of view, not a mood board. The brand should be recognisable without its name.
05
Sell-through
Does it work at ₹8–25K in this city, within 60 days? Commercial viability is not optional.
06
Operational discipline
Fulfilment in 7 days. Inventory clarity. 60-day review readiness. Great product and operational chaos are not a trade we make.
The floor is structured, not just curated.
Taste without commercial architecture is a gallery. We are a store. Every buying decision maps to a specific slot in the floor structure below — and is held accountable to it.
60%
Sell-through drivers
High-conversion pieces. Wearable, wanted, priced right for Mumbai. The commercial spine.
30%
Directional
Pulling the conversation forward. May sell slowly but define what the floor stands for.
10%
Experimental edge
Pure editorial. Here because the argument needs it — not because it will clear in 60 days.
