§ 01 — What We Believe
India doesn't imitate anything.
It leads something.

For decades, Indian fashion operated in two modes: the heritage mode — reviving craft traditions, celebrating the artisan, looking backward — and the aspiration mode, looking toward Paris, Milan, and New York for what "modern" was supposed to mean. Both modes were, in their own way, deferential. Both assumed that the reference point was elsewhere.

That moment is over. A generation of Indian designers has emerged who don't look to Paris for permission and don't look to the past for identity. They look at the world and think: we can do something with that. Something that could only have come from here. Kartik Research on Orchard Street. Péro in 350 stores across 35 countries. Dhruv Kapoor at 40+ global stockists. Suket Dhir, International Woolmark Prize winner. The evidence is overwhelming.

And-a-Man exists to give this movement a home in India — a curated, permanent, beautifully edited space where the designers defining the new Indian design voice are presented together for the first time. We are not a craft store and we are not a luxury department store. We are the platform that was always missing — the one that treats Indian design with the same editorial seriousness that Colette gave French design, that Dover Street Market gave conceptual fashion.

The India the world is beginning to understand has been here all along. We're just making it impossible to miss.

§ 02 — The Three Lenses
Every brand we carry
must pass three tests.
01
Made with Intelligence
The Quality Floor
The product is made with genuine material and process knowledge. This could be handloom mastery. Natural dye expertise. Innovative textile exploration. Exceptional construction. But never generic commercial fabric with no craft thinking behind it.

This lens sets the quality floor, not the identity definition. We are not saying every brand must be "craft-based" in the traditional sense. We are saying every brand must know what it's made of — and why. A designer using a technical fabric in a conceptually rigorous way passes this test. A brand using polyester because it's cheap does not.
The test Ask the designer: why this fabric, why this process? If the answer is specific, intelligent, and reveals material thinking — they're in conversation. If the answer is generic, the brand is not ready for us.
02
Wears like the World
The Silhouette Standard
Every piece must be wearable in 2026 by someone whose wardrobe also includes Lemaire, Auralee, Loewe. Not occasion-wear. Not costume. Clothes for the life your customer actually lives — in Mumbai on a Tuesday, in London at a gallery opening, in New York at a dinner.

This is not about looking Western. It is about looking current — which is a different thing entirely. The Indian designer who has mastered this is not imitating anything. They are creating a silhouette that belongs to right now, made with materials and knowledge that are irreplaceable. That combination is precisely what the global fashion market is looking for and has not yet found enough of.
The test Could this piece coexist in a wardrobe with the best international labels — not as an "ethnic" piece, but as a design equal? If yes, we're interested. If it only makes sense at a wedding or festival, it isn't for And-a-Man.
03
Sounds like India
The Voice Test
The designer's point of view is specifically, irreducibly shaped by being Indian. Not because they use Indian motifs or Indian fabrics. Because the way they see beauty, proportion, colour, the body — it is formed by an Indian sensibility, expressed with contemporary confidence.

No apology. No nostalgia. No imitation. Just the clear signal: this came from here, and here is somewhere. This is the hardest lens to articulate and the easiest to feel. Remove the label from the garment. Is the Indian authorship still visible? Not as decoration, but as a way of thinking? If yes, it passes.
The test Remove the label. Remove the brand story. Is there something in the garment itself — the proportion, the palette, the way it sits on a body — that could only have come from an Indian design intelligence? If that's visible without explanation, it belongs here.
§ 03 — The Boundaries
Curation is defined as much
by exclusion as inclusion.
What And-a-Man is not
Not a craft archiveReverence for Indian textile tradition is not sufficient reason to carry a brand. Beautiful weaving with no design point of view does not earn a place here.
Not a heritage boutiqueWe are not selling the past. We carry the past only when a designer has done the work of translating it into something alive right now.
Not costume occasion-wearWe are not a bridal house or a festival edit. But we carry occasion that feels like real life — elevated, considered, worn once for a wedding and again on a Tuesday. If a brand's product only works at an event and nowhere else, it is not for us. The test is not the occasion; it is whether the garment has a life beyond it.
Not a lesser alternative to Western luxuryWe do not position Indian design as a value-for-money substitute for Loewe or The Row. We position it as a different and equal kind of excellence.
Not a generic sustainable fashion storeEnvironmental ethics are good. They are not, alone, a curation criterion. A brand whose identity is "sustainable fashion made in India" without a specific Indian design voice does not pass the third lens.
What And-a-Man is
A living editorialEvery season's floor is a single coherent statement. The edit evolves based on sell-through, repeat demand, and cultural relevance — in that order. Brands that strengthen the argument and move product stay. Brands that do one without the other are reviewed every 60 days.
A platform for India's design voiceWe carry designers who are showing the world what Indian design looks like at its most confident. The edit is the proof that this voice is real, varied, and world-class.
Globally fluent, specifically IndianOur designers speak the language of contemporary global fashion and have a point of view that is irreducibly Indian. Both simultaneously. Neither in compromise of the other.
Taste with disciplineCulture builds desire. Numbers keep the doors open. Every decision — which brand to carry, which event to programme, which capsule to drop — is held against both tests simultaneously.
The trusted edit for a fatigued market1,800+ Indian fashion brands = search fatigue. We are the answer to the question every culturally-engaged Indian consumer is asking: who do I trust to show me the best of this?
§ 04 — The Practical Filter
Six questions we ask
every brand.
01
What is it made of, and why?
We are looking for a specific, intelligent answer — not "it's sustainable" or "it's handmade." The answer should reveal that the designer thinks in materials. Vague answers fail this test immediately.
02
Could a Lemaire customer wear this on a Tuesday?
Our shorthand test for silhouette modernity and wearability. Not "is this Lemaire?" but "could the same person who loves Lemaire also love this?" If yes, we're interested.
03
Remove the label. Is India still in the room?
The Indian design intelligence must be visible in the garment itself — not just in the brand story or the swing tag. If the Indianness disappears without the label, the brand fails the third lens.
04
Does the designer have a genuine point of view?
Not a mood board. Not a trend response. A specific, personal, defensible way of seeing the world — one that would produce a recognisable design language even if the brand name changed.
05
Can the brand operate with us professionally?
Consignment model requires fulfilment within 7 working days, inventory discipline, and 60-day performance review readiness. Great product and operational chaos are not a trade we make.
06
Does the floor get stronger with this brand in it?
No brand is assessed in isolation. Does it add a dimension — in material, silhouette, price point, geography, or gender balance — that the edit currently lacks? Or does it double up on a mood we already have?
07
Does this sell at our price band, in our city, within 30 days?
Culture builds desire. Numbers keep the doors open. A brand that fails this test is not rejected — it is reclassified. Editorial content earns its place through pull and conversation. Retail inventory earns its place through sell-through. Both have a role. Neither is confused for the other.
